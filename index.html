Schrödinger's Cat, Explained Like a Pro

Schrödinger's cat is a thought experiment by physicist Erwin Schrödinger (1935), not a real pet in a lab. It illustrates how quantum particles can exist in multiple states at once—until observation forces one outcome.

Superposition: two possibilities coexist.
Measurement: the moment of observation.
Collapse: one reality is selected.

What happens in the thought experiment?

  1. A cat is placed in a sealed box with a radioactive source and a detector mechanism.
  2. If decay occurs, a trigger releases poison; if decay does not occur, the cat remains alive.
  3. Before opening the box, quantum interpretation describes the atom as both decayed and not decayed.
  4. So the cat becomes a symbol of a mixed state until observation is made.

Why scientists care

It challenges where quantum behavior ends and classical reality begins.

Common misunderstanding

It is a critique of interpretation, not a suggested real-world experiment.
